A convention in mountain markets says you should list a ski home in the summer — clear access, green lawns, easy photography, soft light. On paper, it makes sense. In practice, we've consistently produced higher outcomes listing during ski season than after it.

Here is why.

**The buyer pool is in town.** Sun Valley receives roughly 65% of its annual high-net-worth visitor traffic between mid-December and mid-March. Those visitors are actively renting homes, eating in the restaurants, skiing the mountain, and — critically — asking local agents whether anything like the rental is for sale. A listing that goes live in February benefits from this in a way no summer listing ever does.

**The home looks like itself.** An alpine home photographed in full summer greenery often doesn't resemble the home a buyer will actually occupy most of the time. Snow on the roof, smoke from the chimney, Nordic skis leaning by the mudroom door — these are how the house *is*, not how it looks on its best day. We've found that showing a home in winter closes the gap between aspiration and reality, and buyers who fall in love under those conditions tend to be less reactive to later discoveries.

**The calendar works against summer.** Summer listings fight for attention with the River Run concerts, polo in Hailey, the Allen & Co. Sun Valley Conference in July, and a dozen other distractions. Winter is quieter, which — counterintuitively — means your listing gets more focused attention from serious buyers.

There are exceptions. Summer listings sometimes make more sense for properties where the outdoor programming is the primary selling feature (large ranches, waterfront access, significant equestrian facilities). But for the alpine modern and mountain contemporary homes that make up most of our Wood River Valley roster, February and early March remain our preferred window.

If you're thinking about listing a valley home in the next 12 months, it is not too early to start the conversation — and if you're reading this in February, it is not too late.
