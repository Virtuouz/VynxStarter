The most common question I field from out-of-state buyers looking at Lake Coeur d'Alene is some variation of: *Does the dock come with the house?*

The answer is almost always "it depends," and the reasons it depends are not typically disclosed in a title commitment.

Idaho law treats navigable lakes as state-owned up to the ordinary high-water mark. That means — regardless of what the deed says — the lakebed itself is not yours. What you do hold, if the property is configured correctly, is a state-issued *encroachment permit* that authorizes a specific dock or mooring structure at that location.

Encroachment permits are grandfathered by use, not by sale. When a waterfront home changes hands, the permit does not automatically transfer. It usually transfers, but it has to be *requested*, and the new owner has sixty days to do so. If the prior owner altered the dock beyond permitted dimensions, the new owner may inherit a compliance issue they didn't know about.

A few practical things to ask for during due diligence:

1. A copy of the current state encroachment permit, not just the county-level shoreline permit.
2. Photographs of the dock at low-water conditions (late winter / early spring) — many issues become visible only when the water drops.
3. Confirmation that the boat lift and any mooring structures were covered under the original permit.
4. Whether any neighbor has ever filed a dispute on shoreline line-of-sight — these are public record, but title does not surface them.

We've closed enough transactions on this lake that we now handle this checklist in-house during the contingency period. On larger transactions, we often bring in a shoreline attorney before a final decision is made.

None of this should deter you from buying on the lake. But going in with clear eyes saves a lot of later friction.
